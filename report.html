Introduction and motivation

This study has utilized two independent data sets, the first represents national productivity through GDP per Hour Worked (GDPHRS) in U.S. Dollars using Purchasing Power Parity (PPP). The second measures the gender wage gap through the difference between the median wages earned by males and females, expressed as a percentage of the median earnings of males. These are important indicators of societal inequities and are significant because they represent large-scale social issues. In selecting the GDP per Hour Worked (GDPHRS), we were motivated to select a precision measure of labor efficiency. In contrast to GDP per capita (which divides the total amount of goods produced and services provided by the entire population) GDP per hour worked measures the exact amount of goods and services produced per hour of work. We chose to use USD Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) to account for differences in the cost of living and inflation rates so that a given unit of output produced in Colombia can be compared to a similar unit produced in Luxembourg in terms of its ability to purchase goods and services. The gender wage gap is defined as the difference between the median wage earned by males and females divided by the median wage earned by males and then multiplied by 100. This is a key macro indicator of inequality; however, this metric is unadjusted and does not consider the potential differences in occupations, education, or hours worked between genders. Therefore, it primarily addresses structural and systemic differences in earning potential based upon gender within the larger economy. A positive number means males earn more than females, while a negative number means females earn more than males. To answer a very important question regarding the relationship between gender wage disparity and national productivity, we plan to graph the gender wage gap against GDP per Hour Worked. Through this graphical analysis, we hope to determine if there is a correlation between high national economic productivity and reduced gender wage disparities. Additionally, we will examine whether highly productive advanced economies automatically reduce gender-based earning disparities or if the gender wage gap exists regardless of the wealth-generating capability of the economy. And to further investigate the nuances of this relationship, we also categorized the 2022 data into three equal groups based upon their national productivity (Lower-Third, Middle-Third, and Top-Third GDP per Hour Worked). A box-and-whisker plot overlaid with individual data points was created to analyze the distribution, median, and variance of the Gender Wage Gap within each of these distinct economic categories. This graphical analysis provides a different perspective by focusing on the concentration and spread of the wage disparities across distinct levels of economic efficiency, rather than a continuous trend line.

A comparison of the time-series animated mapping of the Gender Wage Gap vs. GDP per Hour Worked to the categorical boxplot illustrates many patterns that call into question an assumption of economic development resolving inequality. One of the first observations that comes out of looking at the data is the significant heterogeneity present across both visualizations. For the scatter plot, there is no direct linear correlation between how economically productive each country is and how equitably they distribute income between genders. For example, although South Korea (KOR) has an extremely high level of productivity ($53 USD PPP/hour), it has the largest gap in the dataset at over 31%, while high-productivity European countries like Belgium (BEL) have gaps that range just under 1%. While the scatter plot shows this lack of linear correlation, the boxplot reveals that this variance is heavily concentrated in the lower-third productivity tier. In this group, there is a spread containing both that big South Korean gap and near-perfect parity in countries like Bulgaria (BGR) at 0.5%. Both graphs highlight the unique case of Luxembourg (LUX), which boasts the highest productivity level ($126 USD PPP/hour) and the dataset’s only negative wage gap (-8.6%), showing that the average wage of women is higher than the average wage of men. However, as we look across the boxplot to the top-third productivity group, we see a visibly lower median wage gap (9.0% compared to the lower-third’s 11.7%) and a tighter distribution, with no top-tier country exceeding a 17.5% gap. These findings raise important questions about the data. From the scatter plot’s perspective, why do countries with lower levels of productivity, i.e., Colombia (COL) and Costa Rica (CRI), show very small gaps (less than 2%)? Is the wage gap reflecting a true difference in the treatment of women and men in the workplace, or is it simply that neither gender has access to high-paying jobs, making the wage distribution so flat that everyone makes the same amount of money regardless of gender? Conversely, looking at the boxplot;does reaching the highest echelon of national productivity serve as a systemic “ceiling” that naturally restricts extreme wage disparities? Is the wide variance in the lower-third reflecting the chaotic transition periods of developing economies, while the tightly clustered middle and top tiers imply that advanced economies have adopted similar, standardized labor policies? Furthermore, the large gaps (>20%) in highly productive countries like Japan (JPN) and Israel (ISR) on the scatter plot, combined with the stubborn baseline of inequality (around 9-12%) in the top-tier boxplot, indicate that there may be some deep-rooted cultural beliefs, occupational separation, or a shared “motherhood penalty” that creates barriers to equal opportunity that even extreme wealth cannot erase. The most important thing to remember when using this data to make conclusions is to avoid making causal statements about the relationships shown in the graphs. Neither the scatter plot nor the boxplot indicates that an increase in a country’s Gross Domestic Product (GDP) per hour worked, or moving a country from the lower-third into the top-third of productivity, causes the gender wage gap to magically shrink or grow. They show that a country’s ability to produce goods and services, and the dispersion of its wage inequality, look very different depending on the macroeconomic tier, but the two remain separate issues. They are interrelated through many factors, including but not limited to, the country’s legislative policies, childcare availability, and deep-rooted social norms/history. The data, and only the data, suggests that while the highest productivity tier exhibits a moderately lower median wage gap and fewer extreme outliers, generating wealth is not sufficient to guarantee equal pay for equal work.

def get_from_url(url: str,
                 filename: str,
                 verbose: bool =False, **_) -> pd.DataFrame:
    """Read, write locally, and parse with pandas a remote csv from its url.
    Args:
        url (str): The data's url.
        filename (str): The filename where the data will be written. Should be a .csv file.
    Returns:
        pd.DataFrame: The data inside a dataframe.
    """
    # headers = {"User-Agent": "Mozilla/5.0 (X11; Ubuntu; Linux x86_64; rv:136.0) Gecko/20100101 Firefox/136.0"}

    if verbose:
        print("fetching", url, "...")
    with requests.get(url, stream=True) as response:
        response.raise_for_status()
        with open(filename, "wb") as csv:
            for chunk in response.iter_content(4096):
                csv.write(chunk)
        df = pd.read_csv(filename)
    return df

def get_data(url: str, filename: str, force_refresh: bool=False, verbose: bool =False, **kwargs) -> pd.DataFrame:
    if verbose: print("getting dataset:", url, "file:", filename)
    if force_refresh or not os.path.isfile(filename):
        return get_from_url(url, "_data/"+filename, verbose=verbose, **kwargs)
    return pd.read_csv("_data/" + filename)
